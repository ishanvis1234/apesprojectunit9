⚠️ Bad News

Alaska is Thawing and Rivers Are Turning Toxic

🧊 Background: Alaska's Permafrost Crisis

Permafrost is ground that remains at or below 0 degrees Celsius for at least two consecutive years. In Alaska, much of this ground has been frozen for thousands of years. However, due to accelerating climate change, permafrost is now thawing faster than scientists initially predicted. As it melts, it releases heavy metals including mercury, arsenic, iron, and zinc that had been locked underground for centuries.


These metals flow into rivers and streams, turning the water a rusty orange color and making it toxic to fish, wildlife, and the Indigenous communities that rely on those waterways for drinking water and food. Researchers have documented contamination in more than 75 rivers across Alaska, and the problem continues to spread.


Key Fact: Permafrost stores approximately 1,700 gigatons of carbon worldwide. When it thaws, it releases not only metals into rivers, but also large amounts of CO2 and methane into the atmosphere, further accelerating global warming.

🌍25% of the Northern Hemisphere is permafrost
🏞️75+ Alaskan rivers are contaminated
🌡️Arctic warms 4x faster than the rest of Earth
💨1,700 Gt of carbon locked in permafrost

🔬 Connection to Unit 9: Climate Change

This story illustrates several core Unit 9 concepts occurring simultaneously in a real-world context:

☁️

The Greenhouse Effect

CO2 and methane trap heat in the atmosphere, warming the Arctic. That warming accelerates permafrost thaw, which releases more greenhouse gases. This cycle amplifying itself is what makes it a positive feedback loop.

📈

Increase in Greenhouse Gases

Thawing permafrost releases CO2 and methane that had been stored underground for thousands of years. These emissions add on top of the greenhouse gases already produced by human activities like burning fossil fuels and deforestation.

🌡️

Global Climate Change

The Arctic warms approximately four times faster than the global average due to the albedo feedback effect. As ice melts, darker ground and ocean surfaces are exposed, absorbing more solar radiation and causing additional warming.

Positive Feedback Loop: Arctic Amplification

Rising Global Temps
→
Permafrost Thaws
→
CH4 and CO2 Released
→
More GHGs in Atmosphere
→
Even More Warming
→
Cycle Repeats

A positive feedback loop does not mean something good is happening. It means the effect makes its own cause worse. As warming increases, more permafrost thaws. As more permafrost thaws, additional greenhouse gases are released, which drives even more warming.

⚡ Causes and Environmental Effects

What Causes Permafrost to Thaw
  • Burning fossil fuels raises CO2 in the atmosphere
  • Industrial and agricultural methane emissions
  • Deforestation removes Earth's natural carbon sinks
  • The Arctic albedo feedback loop speeds up regional warming
  • Black carbon from soot darkens snow and ice
⇒
What Happens Because of It
  • Mercury and arsenic flow into rivers from melted ground
  • Fish populations crash in affected waterways
  • Indigenous communities lose clean water and food
  • More CO2 and methane are released, accelerating warming
  • Ground collapses damage roads, pipelines, and buildings
  • Habitat loss for caribou, polar bears, and other Arctic species

📊 Scientific Data and Evidence

Rising CO2 Levels (NOAA) Carbon dioxide in the atmosphere has been climbing steadily for decades. Higher CO2 directly drives the warming that is melting permafrost across Alaska and Siberia. Source: Scripps Institution of Oceanography & NOAA
Where Permafrost Exists This map shows how much of the Northern Hemisphere is covered by permafrost. As temperatures rise, the dark purple areas are thawing and releasing stored carbon and toxic metals. Source: International Permafrost Association (IPA) & USGS
Global Average Temperature Rise (NASA) Temperatures have risen by over 1°C since the Industrial Revolution. The Arctic warms significantly faster than anywhere else, which is why permafrost thaw is accelerating. Source: NASA Goddard Institute for Space Studies (GISS)
Thermokarst Thaw Ponds (Hudson Bay) These ponds form when permafrost melts and the ground collapses. They expand as warming continues, releasing stored carbon and disrupting the surrounding ecosystem. Source: Natural Resources Canada
☣️
Why the Metals Are Dangerous Mercury and arsenic are highly toxic even at low concentrations. They accumulate in fish tissue through bioaccumulation, which means communities that consume fish from contaminated rivers face significant health risks including neurological damage and increased cancer risk.

📝 Key Terms

🧊 Permafrost
Ground that stays at or below 0°C for at least two years. Found mostly in Alaska, Siberia, and northern Canada.
☁️ Greenhouse Gas
Gases like CO2 and methane that trap heat in the atmosphere, warming the planet over time.
🌀 Thermokarst
Sunken terrain like sinkholes and ponds that form when permafrost thaws and the ground collapses.
🔄 Positive Feedback Loop
A cycle where the effect makes the cause worse. Permafrost thaw releases methane, causing more warming, which causes more thaw.
☠️ Heavy Metal Pollutant
Toxic metals like mercury and arsenic that harm living things even at very low concentrations and build up in food chains.
☀️ Albedo
The reflectivity of a surface. Ice reflects sunlight. Dark soil absorbs heat. When ice melts, more heat gets absorbed.
🌳 Carbon Sink
Something that absorbs more carbon than it releases. Frozen permafrost is a sink. When it thaws, it becomes a carbon source.
🐟 Bioaccumulation
The buildup of a toxic substance in an organism over time. Toxic metals from thawed permafrost can build up all the way up a food chain.

⚖️ Environmental Laws and Policy

Clean Water Act (1972)

This law regulates pollutant discharge into U.S. waterways and establishes water quality standards. The heavy metals released by permafrost thaw present a challenge because they originate from a process that, while natural, has been significantly accelerated by human-caused climate change. The EPA actively monitors contamination in affected Alaskan rivers under this law.

Safe Drinking Water Act (1974)

This law establishes federal standards for safe public drinking water in the United States. Indigenous communities in Alaska that depend on now-contaminated rivers face ongoing violations of these standards, creating a serious public health concern that has yet to be fully addressed.

Paris Agreement (2015)

An international treaty in which participating countries committed to limiting global temperature rise to 1.5 to 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. Achieving those targets would significantly reduce the rate of permafrost thaw. However, current national commitments are not sufficient to meet them.

Inflation Reduction Act (2022)

The largest climate investment in U.S. history, directing $369 billion toward reducing greenhouse gas emissions. Lowering emissions is the most direct approach to slowing the Arctic warming that is driving permafrost thaw across Alaska.

📅 Alaska Permafrost: Key Scientific Milestones

2003

IPCC Third Assessment identifies Arctic permafrost as highly vulnerable to accelerating climate change.

2012

Arctic sea ice reaches a record minimum extent, accelerating tundra and permafrost warming rates across the region.

2018

Research teams first document widespread orange river discoloration across Alaska, later linked to permafrost thaw releasing iron and other metals.

2020

Studies confirm permafrost thaw as the primary driver of mercury and heavy metal contamination in Arctic waterways.

2023

Scientists document contamination in more than 75 Alaskan rivers. Indigenous communities formally raise concerns about drinking water safety and subsistence food sources.

2024

The EPA expands monitoring programs for heavy metal contamination in affected river systems across Alaska.

✅ Good News

The Ocean Cleanup Project

🌊 Background: The Ocean Cleanup Project

The Ocean Cleanup is a nonprofit organization founded in 2013 by Boyan Slat, who was 18 at the time. He developed the idea after a scuba diving trip in Greece where he noticed large amounts of plastic floating in the water. The organization's goal is to remove 90 percent of floating ocean plastic by 2040. To accomplish this, they deploy large U-shaped floating barriers in open ocean waters and solar-powered Interceptor vessels in rivers to capture plastic before it reaches the sea.


As of 2025, the organization has completed 54 cleanup operations across 24 countries and removed more than 47 tons of plastic. Ocean plastic is harmful not only because of its physical presence, but because it breaks down into microplastics that enter food chains, causes injury to marine animals, and transports chemical pollutants through ocean ecosystems.


Why This Is Significant: Estimates suggest there are approximately 5.25 trillion pieces of plastic in the world's oceans right now. Plastic pollution threatens over 700 marine species and is responsible for an estimated 100,000 marine mammal and sea turtle deaths each year. The Ocean Cleanup is proving that making a real dent in this problem is possible.

🗑️47.4 tons of plastic removed
🌍24 countries cleaned up
🔢54 cleanup operations completed
🐠700+ marine species threatened by plastic

🔬 Connection to Unit 9: Biodiversity and Human Impacts

The Ocean Cleanup addresses several of the core Unit 9 topics in a direct and measurable way:

🐢

Endangered Species

Sea turtles, whale sharks, and seabirds are all threatened partly because of plastic ingestion and entanglement. Removing plastic directly reduces one of the biggest human-caused threats to these species.

🦜

Human Impacts on Biodiversity

Plastic pollution is one of the most visible examples of human activity damaging biodiversity. The Ocean Cleanup shows that it is possible to actively reduce that harm and support the recovery of ocean ecosystems.

🧪

Ocean Acidification

Plastic pollution and ocean acidification are both human-caused stressors on marine life. Removing plastic takes some of the pressure off species already struggling with lower ocean pH levels.

⚙️
How the Technology Works The Ocean Cleanup uses passive U-shaped floating barriers that move with ocean currents to concentrate plastic into a central collection zone. In rivers, solar-powered Interceptor vessels capture plastic before it reaches the ocean. All recovered plastic is sorted and recycled.

⚡ Causes of the Problem and Effects of Cleanup

Why Ocean Plastic Pollution Happens
  • 8 million tons of single-use plastic enter the ocean every year
  • Poor waste management in many developing countries
  • Rivers carry plastic from inland areas directly to the sea
  • Ghost nets make up about 46% of the Great Pacific Garbage Patch
  • Very few laws hold plastic producers responsible for cleanup
⇒
What Happens When We Remove Plastic
  • Marine animals face fewer entanglement and ingestion hazards
  • Fewer microplastics enter ocean food chains
  • Coral reef ecosystems suffer less physical damage
  • Deaths of seabirds and marine mammals from plastic decrease
  • Coastal communities get cleaner water and better fisheries
  • Recycled plastic creates value and reduces the need for new plastic

📊 Scientific Data and Evidence

Microplastics in Ocean Sediments Plastic fragments smaller than 5mm are now found in every ocean, in drinking water, and even in human blood. Removing large plastic before it breaks down is the key to stopping this. Source: NOAA Marine Debris Program
Ghost Nets Trap Marine Life Ghost nets make up about 46% of the Great Pacific Garbage Patch by weight. They keep trapping and killing marine animals for decades after being lost or thrown away. Source: World Animal Protection & Ocean Conservancy
Marine Species at Risk Sea turtles are among the 700+ marine species threatened by plastic pollution. They frequently ingest plastic bags, mistaking them for jellyfish, which is often fatal. This is one of the most well-documented examples of plastic's direct impact on wildlife. Source: IUCN Red List & WWF Ocean Report
The Great Pacific Garbage Patch This map shows where plastic debris concentrates in the Pacific Ocean. The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is not a solid island of trash, but rather trillions of small plastic fragments distributed across an area roughly twice the size of Texas. Source: The Ocean Cleanup Research Team, 2017
Rising Ocean Heat Content (NOAA) The world's oceans have absorbed over 90% of the excess heat from global warming since 1955. Warmer oceans stress marine ecosystems and accelerate plastic degradation into microplastics, compounding the threat to biodiversity. Source: NOAA Ocean Climate Laboratory

📝 Key Terms

🔬 Microplastics
Plastic particles smaller than 5mm made when larger plastics break down. Now found in oceans, rivers, tap water, food, and human tissue.
🐠 Marine Biodiversity
The variety of living organisms in the ocean. Plastic pollution threatens this through physical injury, chemical poisoning, and habitat damage.
🌀 Ocean Gyre
A large circular ocean current. Gyres concentrate floating debris into garbage patches like the Great Pacific Garbage Patch near Hawaii.
🔺 Biomagnification
When toxins get more concentrated as they move up the food chain. Plastic chemicals biomagnify all the way up to large predators.
🕸️ Ghost Net
Abandoned fishing nets that keep drifting in the ocean and trapping marine animals long after being thrown away.
📍 Point Source Pollution
Pollution from one identifiable location. Rivers act as point sources, channeling plastic waste from land directly into the ocean.
🧹 Environmental Remediation
Cleaning up pollution from an environment. The Ocean Cleanup is one of the biggest marine remediation projects ever attempted.
📦 Extended Producer Responsibility
A policy where companies that make plastic products are required to manage that plastic at the end of its life, which encourages less waste.

⚖️ Environmental Laws and Policy

MARPOL Annex V (1988)

An international marine treaty that prohibits ships from dumping plastics into the ocean. It was among the first global legal efforts to address ocean plastic pollution, though enforcement on the open ocean remains a significant challenge.

Save Our Seas Act (2018, USA)

A U.S. law focused on the prevention and removal of marine debris. It authorized NOAA to coordinate with international partners on ocean cleanup efforts and expand research into the effects of plastic pollution on marine ecosystems.

Save Our Seas 2.0 Act (2020, USA)

An update to the 2018 legislation that introduced stronger requirements for reducing plastic waste at its source and improving waste management infrastructure in developing countries before plastic reaches waterways.

UN Global Plastics Treaty (In Progress, 2025)

A proposed international agreement that would establish legally binding global rules on plastic production, use, and disposal. Negotiations are ongoing as of 2025. If adopted, it would represent the most significant coordinated global action against plastic pollution to date.

🔗 Cross-Unit Synthesis

⚠️ Bad News

Alaska Permafrost: Unit 9 and Unit 7

The Alaska permafrost story connects Unit 9 (Global Climate Change) directly to Unit 7 (Atmospheric Pollution).

Unit 9: Climate Change Unit 7: Atm. Pollution

When permafrost thaws, it releases methane, which is approximately 80 times more potent than CO2 as a greenhouse gas over a 20-year period. This makes it a major contributor to atmospheric pollution on a global scale, which is exactly what Unit 7 covers.

The connection between these two units results in a continuous cycle. Climate change (Unit 9) warms the Arctic and causes permafrost to melt, which releases methane into the atmosphere (Unit 7), which then accelerates further climate change (Unit 9).

Cross-Unit Connection: Permafrost thaw illustrates how a Unit 7 issue (atmospheric pollution) and a Unit 9 issue (climate change) can simultaneously intensify one another.

✅ Good News

The Ocean Cleanup: Unit 9 and Unit 8

The Ocean Cleanup connects Unit 9 (Human Impacts on Biodiversity) directly to Unit 8 (Aquatic Ecosystems).

Unit 9: Biodiversity Unit 8: Aquatic Ecosystems

Unit 8 covers how aquatic ecosystems function, from phytoplankton as primary producers all the way up to apex predators such as sharks and orcas. Plastic pollution disrupts these food webs by physically injuring organisms, introducing chemical toxins through biomagnification, and damaging critical habitats like coral reefs and seagrass beds.

Removing plastic reduces the strain on these ecosystems and gives them the opportunity to recover. This is directly relevant to both the biodiversity topics in Unit 9 and the ecosystem health principles in Unit 8.

Cross-Unit Connection: Removing plastic does more than protect individual animals. It supports the recovery of entire ocean food webs, which is directly relevant to both Unit 8 ecosystem health and Unit 9 biodiversity.

The Main Idea

The greenhouse gas emissions driving Arctic warming and permafrost thaw are a direct result of human decisions, as are the 8 million tons of plastic entering the ocean each year. At the same time, initiatives like the Ocean Cleanup Project and international climate agreements show that human effort is also capable of reversing environmental damage. Positive environmental change is possible with planned decision making.
