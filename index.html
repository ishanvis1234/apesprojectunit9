⚠️ Bad News

Alaska's Permafrost Is Thawing, Making Rivers Toxic

🧊 Background: Alaska's Permafrost Crisis

Permafrost is a layer of ground which remains below zero degrees Celsius for at least two consecutive years. The permafrost present in Alaska has been frozen for several thousand years, and is thawing faster than it naturally should. When it melts, metals like mercury, arsenic, iron, and zinc are released into surrounding bodies of water.


This causes the water to turn orange, and contamination is present in over 75 rivers in Alaska. As a result, fish populations are declining and Indigenous communities face detrimental health consequences, as they rely on these rivers for water and food.


Key Fact: Permafrost contains 1,700 Gt of carbon. As it thaws, this immense amount of CO2 is released into the atmosphere, carrying toxic metals into surrounding waters.

🌍25% of the Northern Hemisphere is permafrost
🏞️75+ Alaskan rivers are contaminated
🌡️Arctic warms 4x faster than the rest of Earth
💨1,700 Gt of carbon locked in permafrost

🔬 Connection to Unit 9: Climate Change

☁️

The Greenhouse Effect

When CO2 and methane are trapped in the atmosphere the temperature begins to rise. This occurs in Alaska as the melting permafrost releases more greenhouse gases, causing more warming.

📈

Increase in Greenhouse Gases

As the permafrost thaws, the greenhouse gases previously trapped for thousands of years are released, adding to the emissions already being produced by other human activities like fossil fuel burning and deforestation. This causes climate change to become more intense due to the abundance of CO2 and methane.

🌡️

Global Climate Change

The Arctic experiences this warming at a pace around four times faster than the global average, due to the albedo effect. As the snow and ice melt, the dark colors of the soil and ocean become more visible. This causes surfaces to absorb more heat, rather than reflecting it as light colors would, resulting in temperatures increasing.

Positive Feedback Loop: Arctic Amplification

Rising Global Temps
→
Permafrost Thaws
→
CH4 and CO2 Released
→
More GHGs in Atmosphere
→
Even More Warming
→
Cycle Repeats

⚡ Causes and Environmental Effects

What Causes Permafrost to Thaw
  • Burning fossil fuels raises CO2 in the atmosphere
  • Industrial and agricultural methane emissions
  • Deforestation removes Earth's natural carbon sinks
  • The Arctic albedo feedback loop speeds up regional warming
  • Black carbon from soot darkens snow and ice
⇒
What Happens Because of It
  • Mercury and arsenic flow into rivers from melted ground
  • Fish populations crash in affected waterways
  • Indigenous communities lose clean water and food
  • More CO2 and methane are released, accelerating warming
  • Ground collapses damage roads, pipelines, and buildings
  • Habitat loss for caribou, polar bears, and other Arctic species

📊 Scientific Data and Evidence

Rising CO2 Levels (NOAA) As seen in the graph, over the past couple of decades the CO2 present in the atmosphere has continued to rise. The Keeling Curve displays this growth beginning in 1958. This rise is the main factor for the increase in Arctic temperatures, speeding up the process of melting the permafrost. Source: Scripps Institution of Oceanography & NOAA
Where Permafrost Exists 25% of the Northern Hemisphere contains permafrost. The areas with the darkest color have a higher concentration of permafrost. When temperatures rise, these areas will release the largest amount of carbon and metals into the atmosphere. Source: International Permafrost Association (IPA) & USGS
Global Average Temperature Rise (NASA) The average global temperature has continued to rise by more than one degree Celsius since the Industrial Revolution. Since the Arctic heats up at four times this pace, this small increase is much more intense in that region. Source: NASA Goddard Institute for Space Studies (GISS)
Thermokarst Thaw Ponds (Hudson Bay) As permafrost melts it creates pools in the ground called thermokarst ponds. These grow as temperature rises, breaking down the soil around them and releasing the carbon that was once stored by the soil into the atmosphere. Source: Natural Resources Canada
☣️
Why the Metals Are Dangerous Mercury and arsenic are harmful in small concentrations. They build up in fish tissues, and when consumed by humans, they consume these metals as well. This has detrimental health impacts like nerve and brain damage, being more significant in young children.

📝 Key Terms

🧊 Permafrost
Ground that stays at or below 0°C for at least two years. Found mostly in Alaska, Siberia, and northern Canada.
☁️ Greenhouse Gas
Gases like CO2 and methane that trap heat in the atmosphere, warming the planet over time.
🌀 Thermokarst
Sunken terrain like sinkholes and ponds that form when permafrost thaws and the ground collapses.
🔄 Positive Feedback Loop
A cycle where the effect makes the cause worse. Permafrost thaw releases methane, causing more warming, which causes more thaw.
☠️ Heavy Metal Pollutant
Toxic metals like mercury and arsenic that harm living things even at very low concentrations and build up in food chains.
☀️ Albedo
The reflectivity of a surface. Ice reflects sunlight. Dark soil absorbs heat. When ice melts, more heat gets absorbed.
🌳 Carbon Sink
Something that absorbs more carbon than it releases. Frozen permafrost is a sink. When it thaws, it becomes a carbon source.
🐟 Bioaccumulation
The buildup of a toxic substance in an organism over time. Toxic metals from thawed permafrost can build up all the way up a food chain.

⚖️ Environmental Laws and Policy

Clean Water Act (1972)

The Clean Water Act restricts what can enter U.S. waters, however in Alaska these metals come from the ground thawing, not factory emissions. Even though the EPA monitors this issue, the natural source of emissions makes it difficult to regulate.

Safe Drinking Water Act (1974)

The Safe Drinking Water Act creates federal rules for what water is safe to consume. Indigenous communities in Alaska now have water that does not comply with those standards, causing negative health effects.

Paris Agreement (2015)

The Paris Agreement created a 1.5 to 2 degrees Celsius limit to global warming. By staying within that range, the permafrost thawing will reduce. However, the current regulations and plans in place are not efficient enough to meet that standard.

Inflation Reduction Act (2022)

The Inflation Reduction Act put $369 billion in funding towards cutting emissions and growing clean energy, directly helping slow the Arctic warming that melts permafrost. This is the biggest budget the U.S. government has provided towards helping solve climate change.

📅 Alaska Permafrost: Key Scientific Milestones

The IPCC states that Arctic permafrost thawing will increase rapidly.

Arctic sea ice is at an all-time low, causing more of the dark ocean to be exposed and heat to be absorbed, raising temperatures.

The color of Alaskan rivers turns orange as a result of the metals released from the permafrost thawing.

2020

Researchers confirm that permafrost thawing is the source of river contamination.

2023

Contamination is present in over 75 Alaskan rivers, and Indigenous communities report concerns about using those rivers as a source of food and water.

The EPA begins monitoring the river contamination in Alaska.

✅ Good News

The Ocean Cleanup Project

🌊 Background: The Ocean Cleanup Project

The Ocean Cleanup project began in 2013. The founder thought of this idea after seeing several pieces of plastic during his scuba diving trip in Greece. The organization's objective is to remove 90% of all plastic pollution present in the ocean by 2040. They do this by using U-shaped barriers in the ocean, which push plastic into a confined area. Solar-powered vessels, called Interceptors, then collect the plastic.


During 2025, the Ocean Cleanup project has removed more than 47 tons of plastic. This plastic often injured and trapped marine animals. The microplastics from plastic degrading also entered marine food chains and carried chemical pollutants, harming underwater ecosystems.


Why This Is Significant: There are almost 5.25 trillion pieces of plastic present in the ocean, harming over 700 marine species. Almost 100,000 marine animals die due to this annually.

🗑️47.4 tons of plastic removed
🌍24 countries cleaned up
🔢54 cleanup operations completed
🐠700+ marine species threatened by plastic

🔬 Connection to Unit 9: Biodiversity and Human Impacts

🐢

Endangered Species

Sea turtles, whale sharks, and seabirds are part of the endangered species group as a result of death caused by consuming plastic and becoming trapped in it. By removing this pollution, it reduces one of the main causes of death these species experience, helping grow their population.

🦜

Human Impacts on Biodiversity

Plastic pollution damages biodiversity. It is caused by humans, but can also be physically removed and cleaned up by humans. The Ocean Cleanup project helps work towards resolving this issue.

🧪

Ocean Acidification

Ocean acidification occurs when CO2 dissolves and lowers the pH of seawater, making it difficult for marine life to survive. These species also face the threats of plastic pollution. By reducing this, it removes one of the causes of death for marine life and increases their survival chances.

⚙️
How the Technology Works A U-shaped barrier is moved with ocean currents, pushing plastic debris towards a collection point. Solar-powered Interceptor boats then pull this debris out of the water. The collected material is then sorted and recycled.

⚡ Causes of the Problem and Effects of Cleanup

Why Ocean Plastic Pollution Happens
  • 8 million tons of single-use plastic enter the ocean every year
  • Poor waste management in many developing countries
  • Rivers carry plastic from inland areas directly to the sea
  • Ghost nets make up about 46% of the Great Pacific Garbage Patch
  • Very few laws hold plastic producers responsible for cleanup
⇒
What Happens When We Remove Plastic
  • Marine animals face fewer entanglement and ingestion hazards
  • Fewer microplastics enter ocean food chains
  • Coral reef ecosystems suffer less physical damage
  • Deaths of seabirds and marine mammals from plastic decrease
  • Coastal communities get cleaner water and better fisheries
  • Recycled plastic creates value and reduces the need for new plastic

📊 Scientific Data and Evidence

Microplastics in Ocean Sediments Plastic particles are called microplastics when they are smaller than 5mm. They have been found in all bodies of water and in human blood. By removing large pieces of plastic, we are able to prevent the formation of microplastics which contaminate several things. Source: NOAA Marine Debris Program
Ghost Nets Trap Marine Life Abandoned fishing nets are present in 46% of the Great Pacific Garbage Patch. These nets trap and kill marine animals long after being discarded. Source: World Animal Protection & Ocean Conservancy
Marine Species at Risk Sea turtles consume plastic bags mistaking them for jellyfish, causing deadly internal blockages. This effectively contributes to the decline of the turtle population. Source: IUCN Red List & WWF Ocean Report
The Great Pacific Garbage Patch The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is a large zone of trash that is double the size of Texas. Ocean currents pull plastic pollution into this area, concentrating it across the surface. Source: The Ocean Cleanup Research Team, 2017
Rising Ocean Heat Content (NOAA) Oceans have absorbed 90% of heat from global warming, as seen in the graph's steady uptrend beginning in 1960. The added heat breaks down plastic pollution faster, adding more microplastics to marine ecosystems. Source: NOAA Ocean Climate Laboratory

📝 Key Terms

🔬 Microplastics
Plastic particles smaller than 5mm made when larger plastics break down. Now found in oceans, rivers, tap water, food, and human tissue.
🐠 Marine Biodiversity
The variety of living organisms in the ocean. Plastic pollution threatens this through physical injury, chemical poisoning, and habitat damage.
🌀 Ocean Gyre
A large circular ocean current. Gyres concentrate floating debris into garbage patches like the Great Pacific Garbage Patch near Hawaii.
🔺 Biomagnification
When toxins get more concentrated as they move up the food chain. Plastic chemicals biomagnify all the way up to large predators.
🕸️ Ghost Net
Abandoned fishing nets that keep drifting in the ocean and trapping marine animals after being thrown away.
📍 Point Source Pollution
Pollution from one identifiable location. Rivers act as point sources, channeling plastic waste from land directly into the ocean.
🧹 Environmental Restoration
Cleaning up pollution from an environment. The Ocean Cleanup is one of the biggest marine restoration projects ever attempted.
📦 Extended Producer Responsibility
A policy where companies that make plastic products are required to manage that plastic at the end of its life, which encourages less waste.

⚖️ Environmental Laws and Policy

MARPOL Annex V (1988)

MARPOL Annex V made it illegal to dump plastic in the ocean. It is one of the first agreements that helps resolve ocean plastic pollution, however violations still occur.

Save Our Seas Act (2018, USA)

The Save Our Seas Act requires NOAA to monitor marine debris prevention. It also ensures the U.S. collaborates with other countries to succeed with this goal, cleaning up shared ocean space.

Save Our Seas 2.0 Act (2020, USA)

The 2.0 version of this act targets plastic before it enters the ocean, calling for better waste management in countries where rivers carry large amounts of plastic to the sea.

UN Global Plastics Treaty (In Progress, 2025)

This treaty is still in progress, but would enforce regulations on how plastic is created and disposed of, resulting in a large global effort to target the issue of plastic pollution.

🔗 Cross-Unit Synthesis

⚠️ Bad News

Alaska Permafrost: Unit 9 and Unit 7

Unit 9: Climate Change Unit 7: Atm. Pollution

The thawing of permafrost releases methane which is 80 times stronger than the amount of CO2 built up in a 20-year timeframe. Methane is the most damaging atmospheric pollutant focused on in Unit 7.

Both Unit 9 and Unit 7 are linked by a feedback loop. As permafrost melts, it releases methane. That methane creates more warming, which then melts additional permafrost, forming a cycle.

Cross-Unit Connection: Permafrost thawing is an example in which a Unit 7 pollutant is present in a Unit 9 issue, as both directly make one another worse.

✅ Good News

The Ocean Cleanup: Unit 9 and Unit 8

Unit 9: Biodiversity Unit 8: Aquatic Ecosystems

Aquatic ecosystems are discussed in Unit 8. Plastic impacts the health of these ecosystems from producers to consumers. It injures marine wildlife and causes toxic chemicals to build up in the food chain, along with damaging coral reefs.

By removing plastics, the detrimental effects on ecosystems will be reduced. This will cause fewer species to die from pollution and increase species biodiversity.

Cross-Unit Connection: By removing plastic, the stress on the food web is released. This connects the issue of ecosystem health from Unit 8 to the issue of reduced biodiversity in Unit 9, as when health is increased biodiversity increases as well.

The Main Idea

Both plastic pollution in the ocean and the greenhouse gas emissions causing Arctic warming are a result of human activity. Positive human activity includes the Ocean Cleanup project and several international climate agreements, displaying how humans can help reduce the harm done to ecosystems. Through planned decision making, positive environmental change is possible.
